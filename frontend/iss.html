What that tiny dot is actually doing

The ISS isn't just fast - the speed and altitude it's traveling at have genuinely strange consequences, including a real, measurable effect on time itself for the people inside it.

Time dilation
~0.01 sec

Over a six-month mission, an ISS astronaut's onboard clock ends up running about 0.01 seconds behind a clock on Earth - almost entirely due to the station's orbital speed (special relativity), very slightly offset by the weaker gravity at orbital altitude (general relativity). It's tiny, but it's real, measurable time travel into the future.

Speed
7.66 km/s

That's roughly 27,600 km/h - fast enough to travel from London to New York in about 7 minutes. At that speed, the dot on the globe above can visibly move between refreshes.

Orbit
~92.7 min

One full lap of Earth. That means the ISS completes about 15.5 orbits every single day - more than the number of days in two weeks, every 24 hours.

Sunrises
~16 / day

Because each orbit is so short, astronauts on board experience roughly 16 sunrises and 16 sunsets every day - a day-night cycle about 90 times faster than the one you're in right now.

Visibility
Brighter than most stars

When conditions are right, the ISS reflects enough sunlight to outshine almost every star in the sky - visible to the naked eye as a fast-moving, steady point of light, no telescope required.

Cumulative effect
~0.02 sec

Cosmonaut Sergei Krikalev, who has spent more cumulative time in orbit than almost anyone, is estimated to have aged a few hundredths of a second less than someone who stayed on Earth the whole time - the largest known human time-dilation effect from spaceflight to date.
